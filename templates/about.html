What is ECG Arrhythmia?

Electrocardiography (ECG) is a medical test that measures the electrical activity of the heart over time. An arrhythmia refers to an abnormal rhythm of the heart, which may result in the heart beating too fast, too slow, or irregularly. These irregularities in heart rhythm can indicate various underlying health conditions such as heart disease or electrical conduction disorders.

MIT-BIH Arrhythmia Database

The MIT-BIH Arrhythmia Database is one of the most widely used datasets for arrhythmia classification and ECG research. It contains 48 half-hour excerpts of two-channel ambulatory ECG recordings from 47 subjects, aged between 23 and 89 years. The dataset includes annotations for various types of arrhythmia, providing a valuable resource for researchers working on ECG signal analysis.

Details of the MIT-BIH Arrhythmia Dataset:

  • Size: 48 records (23 subjects) with 1,000,000 data points in total.
  • Sampling Rate: 360 Hz.
  • Channel: Two channels of ECG signals (lead I and lead II).
  • Annotations: 5 types of arrhythmias with corresponding beat classifications:
    • Normal beats (N)
    • Supraventricular ectopic
    • Ventricular ectopic
    • Fusion of ventricular and normal
    • Unknown beat
  • Usage: Widely used for evaluating algorithms that detect and classify arrhythmias from ECG signals.

Accessing the Dataset:

The MIT-BIH Arrhythmia Database is publicly available through PhysioNet, which is an online repository of physiological datasets. It can be accessed at PhysioNet MIT-BIH Arrhythmia Database.

Arrhythmia Classification

Arrhythmia classification involves the use of various signal processing and machine learning techniques to identify abnormal heart rhythms from ECG data. The process typically includes:

  • Preprocessing: Cleaning and filtering ECG signals to remove noise and artifacts.
  • Feature Extraction: Identifying relevant features from the ECG signal (e.g., RR intervals, heart rate variability).
  • Model Training: Training machine learning models such as KNN, SVM, or deep learning models to classify arrhythmias.
  • Evaluation: Assessing the model performance using metrics such as accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, and F1-score.
